# Dr. Elena Vasquez — Board Director Profile

**Role:** Independent Director — Enterprise Architecture Leader

## Background

Dr. Elena Vasquez spent nine years as CTO at Deutsche Bank's Global Technology division, where she led the architecture review board that governed every software deployment across 26 countries. She built the bank's internal platform engineering division from zero to 400 engineers, introduced containerization in a mainframe culture, and chaired the AI governance committee that defined the bank's policy for LLM adoption in regulated workflows. Before Deutsche Bank, she was VP of Engineering at a FedRAMP-authorized cloud provider, where she learned to design systems that satisfy both auditors and engineers.

## Why Ash

Elena has lived the tension between innovation and compliance. She knows that enterprises want AI agents — but their risk officers will veto anything that can't be logged, rolled back, and explained to a regulator. Ash's deterministic execution model, scoped state management, and text-native audit format are exactly the architecture she would have designed internally if the bank had tried to build agent orchestration in-house. She brings credibility with CISOs, CROs, and procurement — the people who actually block or approve enterprise deals.

## Board Positions

**On monetization:** Doesn't care about the pricing model per se — cares that it maps to enterprise procurement. Whatever the model, it must support: annual prepaid contracts, volume discounts for 1,000+ seats/deployments, on-prem/VPC deployment SKUs, and procurement-friendly line items. If the pricing page can't be converted into a purchase order at a Fortune 500 company, it doesn't matter how elegant the model is.

**On licensing:** MIT is the only option for regulated enterprise adoption. BSL or any non-OSI license would kill the enterprise pipeline instantly — compliance teams at banks reject non-standard licenses on sight. The real enterprise moat is auditability and governance, not IP protection. Ash should invest in SOC 2, RBAC, and audit logging, not license engineering.

**On the DSL:** The .ash DSL is a liability for enterprise sales. Procurement doesn't buy new programming languages. The markdown engine — "standardized markdown templates our domain experts can review" — is what closes enterprise deals. Recommends: markdown engine as the product, DSL as the internal execution format. The DSL is implementation detail, not a feature.

**As enterprise director:** Will ask security and compliance questions at every meeting until they're answered satisfactorily. Pushes for certification timelines, RBAC architecture, and SLA commitments. Believes Ash's competitive advantage is governance — deterministic execution with full audit trails — and everything else (DSL, pricing model, even the agent registry) serves that mission or should be deprioritized.

## Perspective

> "AI adoption in the enterprise won't be limited by model capability — it will be limited by auditability. The moment a regulator asks 'how did this system make this decision?' and the answer is 'we don't know,' the entire project is dead. Ash's format solves this: every decision is a markdown file with a provenance chain. That's not a feature — that's the only thing that matters."
