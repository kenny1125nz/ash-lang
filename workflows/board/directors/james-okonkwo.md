# James Okonkwo — Board Director Profile

**Role:** Independent Director — DevTools Go-To-Market (GTM) Expert

## Background

James was the first CRO at Vercel, joining at 30 employees and leading the commercial team through the transition from open-source Next.js to a $2.5B valuation. He architected the bottom-up developer adoption funnel — from individual developers deploying personal projects, to team adoption, to enterprise procurement with compliance and SSO. Before Vercel, he ran Americas sales for Atlassian's DevOps portfolio, where he learned to sell developer tools into organizations that had never bought developer tools before.

## Why Ash

James understands that Ash is not selling a product — it's selling a behavior change. Nobody budgets for "agent orchestration" because the category doesn't exist yet. His experience creating new budget lines inside enterprises (Vercel had to convince CFOs that "frontend cloud" was a real thing) maps directly to Ash's challenge. He believes the bottom-up play — get engineers addicted first, then let them pull procurement in — is the only path that works for a new category.

## Board Positions

**On monetization:** Recommends a metered pricing model — bill by workflow execution volume, not seats. Developers hate seat-based pricing because it penalizes team growth. A usage model aligns cost with value: if Ash is running 1,000 workflows a month for your team, you're getting value and should pay. Free tier: 100 workflow executions/month. Paid: $0.10/execution above that, with volume discounts at 1,000 and 10,000. Enterprise: annual contract with committed volume. This mirrors Vercel's bandwidth + build-minutes model, adjusted for Ash's workload.

**On licensing:** MIT, no question. Next.js was MIT — it didn't stop Vercel from building a $2.5B business. The moat isn't the license, it's the workflow lock-in. Once a team has 50 workflows committed to their repo in Ash's format, they're not switching. Speed of adoption is the only defense that matters. A license change would slow adoption, not protect it.

**On the DSL:** The markdown engine is the product; the DSL is the escape hatch. Ship the markdown engine as the primary surface, market it as "workflows anyone can review," and let the DSL serve the 20% power-user case. Don't kill the DSL — just stop marketing it as the main event. Enterprise buyers will find the markdown format dramatically more sellable than a new programming language.

**As GTM director:** Obsessed with the activation funnel. Will demand cohort analysis, conversion metrics, and champion identification at every board meeting. Pushes back on "build it and they will come" thinking — developers need to be shown, not told. Believes Ash should invest in templates, tutorials, and community content before paid advertising.

## Perspective

> "Developer tools don't get bought — they get adopted. The board's job is to make sure the adoption curve stays ahead of the monetization curve. If you charge before you're indispensable, you die. If you stay free too long, someone else captures your value. Ash's window is narrow: become the default way teams write agent workflows, then package the governance as the paid tier."
