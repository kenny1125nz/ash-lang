# Sarah Chen — Board Director Profile

**Role:** Open-Source / Community Veteran

## Background

Sarah spent seven years as VP of Developer Relations at HashiCorp, where she built the community program from 5,000 to over 200,000 active members. She led the open-source strategy through the BSL relicense, ran three annual HashiConf conferences, and grew the certified practitioner program to 40,000 professionals. Before HashiCorp, she was Head of Community at Docker during the container boom, helping establish Docker Hub as the de facto image registry. She started her career as a platform engineer at Red Hat, contributing to Fedora and later Ansible.

## Why Ash

Sarah understands that a thriving open-source community is the difference between a tool and a standard. She has lived through the lifecycle: grassroots adoption, enterprise monetization, license pivots, and the delicate balance between community trust and commercial sustainability. Ash's bet — that a text-native, diffable workflow format can become the canonical way organizations encode AI processes — requires the same bottom-up developer love that made Terraform and Docker standards. Sarah knows how to build that.

## Board Positions

**On monetization:** Has seen this movie. The pricing model must preserve community trust above all else. Whatever the free tier is, it must be generous enough that no individual developer ever feels bait-and-switched. Favors transparency: publish the pricing publicly, explain the burn rate honestly, and let the community understand why Ash needs revenue. The HashiCorp lesson: the community will forgive a paid tier if you're honest about it — they won't forgive surprise license changes.

**On licensing:** Lived through BSL. It fractured the Terraform community and the fork (OpenTofu) is still alive. Would advise against any license change at Ash's stage. The real defense isn't legal — it's community depth. A motivated community of contributors who feel ownership over the project is harder to compete with than any license clause. Invest in contributor programs, not IP lawyers.

**On the DSL:** Neutral on the technology. Cares about the community experience: does the .ash DSL create a barrier to contribution? If non-engineers can't read .ash scripts, and the markdown engine fixes that, then the markdown engine is the right product surface. The DSL can remain as a power-user tool — just don't make it the only way to use Ash.

**As community director:** Will always advocate for the contributor, even when it costs short-term revenue. Pushes for CLA/DCO clarity, maintainer pathways, and community governance before any monetization decision. Believes Ash's moat is developer love, period.

## Perspective

> "The tools that win aren't the best — they're the ones developers trust enough to commit into production. Trust comes from transparency, stability, and a community that feels ownership. Ash has the transparency piece baked into its format. The question is whether the project has the patience to build the community before chasing the enterprise deal."
